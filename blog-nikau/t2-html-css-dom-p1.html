Analogy: Building an umu

To build an umu you need stones, wood, coconut shells and husk.

The wood and kindling husk is set alight and the stones become hot and food is then placed on top of the stones and covered with leaves.

Baskets are woven and when they are complete, the food should be cooked.

Food is placed with care in the baskets and distributed to neighbouring families in the village.

  • HTML is like the items needed to cook the food; the wood, stones, coconut shells and husk. It acts as a foundation of the whole website 'cooking' process. Without this foundation, you will not be able to cook any delicious food.
  • CSS is like the food placed and cooked on top of the umu foundation which then becomes yummy and flavourful. If the food is not stacked with care on top of the stones and in the correct order, the food may be undercooked or burnt.
  • DOM is like the process of weaving and placing food in baskets and sharing it. This process is like the representation or 'interface' of the relationship you have with your fellow vilagers.

Boxifying design

Boxifying design and the box model are a crucial part of the web page 'cooking' process.

To 'boxify a design' is to break a web design mock up down into smaller boxes. To do this you may:

  • make boxes on an image of the design
  • draw boxes to represent the design freehand
  • print the design and cut it into boxes

These methods are great ways to break down what lays ahead for implementing the design.

The boxify process allows you to have a visual representation on the structure required to build the foundation of the website (or umu).

A great way to understand the layout of web pages is to get familiar with the 'box model'.

In Udacity Lesson 1, I learnt that EVERY element is a rectangular box made made up of a margin, border and padding around content.
